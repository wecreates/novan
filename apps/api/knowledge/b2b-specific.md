# B2B-specific operations — selling to businesses, not consumers

B2B and B2C share the word "business" but almost nothing else. B2B has longer sales cycles, multi-stakeholder decisions, higher LTV per customer, harder distribution, and entirely different content economics. Most operators fail at B2B by trying to run it like B2C — fast iteration on cheap acquisition. The right move is fewer, deeper relationships with persistent business value.

## What the operator should remember

- B2B buying decisions involve 5-12 people on average, not one. The "buyer" is rarely the decision-maker.
- The B2B sales cycle is 30 days at the SMB end, 90-180 days at mid-market, 6-18 months at enterprise. Plan accordingly.
- LTV in B2B is often 10-50x larger than B2C, which justifies entirely different acquisition economics.
- B2B churn at 2% monthly is excellent; B2C SaaS targets are different.
- Content marketing pays back over years, not weeks. A blog post that ranks for a high-intent commercial query can be worth $50k-500k over its lifetime.

## The B2B funnel — different shape than B2C

### Stage 1: Awareness (the buyer doesn't know you exist)
- They're searching for solutions to a problem
- They consume content from authorities in the space (analyst reports, blogs, podcasts)
- Decision-influence: thought leaders, peers, search-engine results
- Operator move: SEO content, podcast appearances, conference talks, free tools

### Stage 2: Consideration (they have a shortlist)
- They're comparing 3-7 vendors
- They want demos, case studies, comparison guides
- Decision-influence: peer reviews (G2, Capterra), case studies, sales conversations
- Operator move: clear product positioning, public case studies, comparison pages, ROI calculators

### Stage 3: Evaluation (they're testing)
- They've narrowed to 2-3 vendors
- They're running pilots, trials, POCs
- Decision-influence: hands-on experience, technical fit, sales support quality
- Operator move: white-glove onboarding, technical office hours, fast support

### Stage 4: Decision (they're picking)
- They've decided what they want
- They're negotiating terms, security review, procurement
- Decision-influence: security/compliance, contract terms, references
- Operator move: streamlined security questionnaires, flexible terms, reference customers

### Stage 5: Expansion (they're growing usage)
- They're buying more seats, adding products, deepening usage
- Decision-influence: account manager relationship, success metrics, expansion offers
- Operator move: dedicated CSM tier above $X ARR, quarterly business reviews, expansion playbook

The brain treats these stages as discrete in the analytics layer; conversion between stages is the funnel.

## The three B2B segment archetypes

### SMB (Small/Medium Business): 1-50 employees
- ACV: $500-$5k/year
- Sales cycle: 7-30 days
- Decision-maker: usually 1-2 people, often the founder
- Acquisition: self-serve + light-touch sales
- Touch point: product-led growth + email + chat
- Renewal: usage-driven, low-touch
- Churn rate target: 5-8% monthly

### Mid-Market: 50-1000 employees
- ACV: $5k-$50k/year
- Sales cycle: 30-90 days
- Decision-maker: department head + IT + procurement (3-5 people)
- Acquisition: marketing + inside sales
- Touch point: demos + custom onboarding + dedicated AM
- Renewal: QBR-driven, account team
- Churn rate target: 1-2% monthly

### Enterprise: 1000+ employees
- ACV: $50k-$1M+/year
- Sales cycle: 6-18 months
- Decision-maker: 5-12 people including C-level, legal, security, IT
- Acquisition: ABM + field sales + outbound
- Touch point: dedicated rep + SE + CSM
- Renewal: multi-year contracts, executive sponsor
- Churn rate target: <1% monthly

Pick ONE archetype per business and build the org around it. The brain's `business.feasibility` analyzer asks for this segment choice explicitly because the economics differ by 10x+.

## Pricing in B2B

### Don't publish enterprise pricing publicly
- "Contact sales" is the right answer above $50k ACV
- Reason: enterprise buying is bespoke; published pricing anchors the conversation badly
- BUT: SMB pricing should be public, transparent, and self-serve

### Volume tiers are standard
- 5-10 seat free tier
- 11-50 seat standard
- 51-500 seat business
- 501+ enterprise (call us)
- Each tier transitions at a logical seat count + adds at least one valuable feature

### Per-seat vs. per-usage
- Per-seat: predictable revenue, easier to forecast, customer-friendly for collaboration tools
- Per-usage: aligned with value but bill-shock risk
- Hybrid: base + overage common in enterprise (best of both)

### Annual prepay
- 10-20% discount for annual prepay
- Improves cash flow significantly
- Reduces churn (sunk cost effect)
- Standard in B2B; SMB customers may decline; mid-market+ usually accept

## B2B content marketing — different from B2C

### What works:
- **Practitioner-focused content** — "How we solved X" written by someone who actually solved it
- **Comparison content** — "Tool A vs Tool B" with honest tradeoffs (Google rewards this; users desperately want it)
- **Templates and tools** — fillable Excel, free calculators, ROI estimators (collect email)
- **Research reports** — proprietary data → press coverage → backlinks → SEO
- **Case studies** — specific customer + specific outcome + specific numbers
- **Industry-specific content** — segment by industry (healthcare CRM differs from legal CRM)

### What doesn't:
- Generic "5 tips" listicles (too crowded; rarely earn rankings)
- "Thought leadership" without distinct point of view
- Pure aspirational copy with no operational detail
- Product-feature blog posts (audience for these is tiny)
- Anything that wouldn't help if someone with zero context found it

### Content cadence:
- 1 cornerstone piece (3000+ words, deeply researched) per month
- 2-4 derivative pieces from each cornerstone (different angles, formats)
- Monthly newsletter rounding up the month's best content
- Weekly LinkedIn presence with the operator's POV

## B2B distribution channels — priority by effectiveness

### High-leverage (start here)
1. **SEO** — high-intent commercial queries; payoff over 12-24 months; compounds forever
2. **LinkedIn** — primary B2B social platform; both organic content and ads
3. **Email** — ABM-style targeted outreach + nurture sequences
4. **Partnerships** — integration partners, channel partners, referrals from adjacent vendors
5. **Industry events** — conferences, podcasts, virtual summits (speaking spots > sponsorships)

### Medium-leverage
6. **Webinars** — pair with email lists; record for evergreen content
7. **Comparison sites** — G2, Capterra, TrustRadius; require maintenance + case studies
8. **Paid LinkedIn / Google search** — high CAC but high quality buyers

### Low-leverage (B2B-specific)
9. **TikTok, Instagram** — wrong demographic for most B2B
10. **Display ads** — generally poor for B2B
11. **SEO for high-volume but low-intent queries** — wastes time

The brain's `social.recommendCadence` reflects this — LinkedIn is the only high-priority social platform for B2B; everything else is supplementary.

## B2B sales process essentials

### Discovery — diagnose before prescribing
- Ask about current state: what's broken, what they're using now
- Ask about consequences: what does the problem cost them today
- Ask about urgency: why now, what's the trigger
- Ask about decision: who else is involved, what's the buying process
- The product/demo conversation comes AFTER discovery, not before

### Demos — show value, not features
- Build the demo around their specific use case (not your generic deck)
- Show outcomes (the report, the result, the saved time) not interfaces
- Encourage them to drive parts of the demo
- Send a follow-up summary within 24 hours

### Proposals — quantify ROI, not just price
- Show their current cost (estimate based on discovery)
- Show your solution's cost
- Show the delta and the payback period
- Anchor on the value, not the price

### Closing — make it easy to say yes
- Standard MSA + order form, not legal-heavy custom contract
- 30-day money-back guarantee (safer than free trial for SMB B2B)
- Flexible terms within reason (quarterly vs. annual, partial year)
- Don't apply discount pressure; let urgency come from their problem

## B2B customer success — different from support

Customer success in B2B is a function, not just a queue.

- **Onboarding** — 30-90 days dedicated success engagement; clear milestones; "first value achieved" event tracked
- **Quarterly Business Reviews (QBR)** — formal meeting with stakeholders; review outcomes, address blockers, surface expansion opportunities
- **Health scoring** — composite indicator (usage trends, support tickets, NPS, executive sponsor engagement)
- **Expansion playbooks** — specific triggers for upsell, cross-sell, multi-year renewal
- **Renewal motion** — start 90 days before renewal; address risk; secure executive sponsor commitment

For mid-market+ accounts, customer success starts BEFORE the contract is signed (in the pilot/POC phase) and is a major retention lever.

## Compliance, security, legal

B2B buyers (especially enterprise) require:

- **SOC 2 Type II** — most enterprise won't even talk without it (12-18 month process; $30-100k cost)
- **GDPR / CCPA compliance** — required for EU / California customers
- **DPA (Data Processing Agreement)** — standard contract addendum
- **MSA (Master Service Agreement)** — your standard contract; negotiate annually
- **Security questionnaires** — VSAQ, CAIQ; pre-fill answers for common ones
- **SSO / SAML** — table stakes for >100-seat customers

The brain monitors for these via the operational-readiness catalog; SOC 2 in particular is a multi-month effort that needs to be scheduled, not started reactively.

## What the brain MUST NOT do

- Run consumer-style growth hacks on B2B audiences (damages credibility)
- Public price-tier disclosure for enterprise pricing without operator approval
- Sign contracts on operator's behalf (no exceptions; legal binding requires human)
- Promise compliance certifications you don't have
- Use customer logos/case studies without explicit consent (legal exposure)
- Run sales emails with fake personalization tokens (gets domain blacklisted)

## What "good" looks like in B2B

For a mid-market SaaS business at $500k ARR:
- 20-50 active customers
- ACV: $10k-$30k average
- Sales cycle: 45-90 days
- Sales-led conversion (demo → close): 25-40%
- Logo retention: 90%+ annually
- NRR: 115%+ (expansion exceeds churn)
- Customer count growth: 50-100% YoY in first 3 years
- Operator's time on customer-success calls: 30-50% (do not stop doing this until $2M ARR)

A B2B operator who tries to manage 200 SMB customers with self-serve only will discover by month 6 that "managing" isn't happening and churn is killing them. Scale headcount or product to support the customer base. Don't drop the relationship motion.
