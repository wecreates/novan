# Community building — turning audience into ecosystem

A community is not a Slack channel with people in it. A community is a group whose members talk to each other (not just to you) about something they care about. The difference matters because audiences are extractive (you broadcast, they consume); communities are generative (members create value for each other). The operator who builds the first transitions from "running a business" to "stewarding an ecosystem" — fundamentally different economics.

## What the operator should remember

- Most communities fail because the host tries to BE the value rather than create the conditions for value among members.
- The first 100 members determine the community's culture for its lifetime. Curate them by hand.
- "Engagement" is a vanity metric. The real metric is whether members find each other valuable.
- A community of 50 deeply engaged members beats a community of 5,000 lurkers for every operator outcome.
- Communities need rituals, not just access. Without rituals (weekly Q&A, monthly call, annual event), they become inboxes.

## Three community archetypes

### 1. Practitioner community
- People who do similar work (designers, developers, real-estate agents, restaurateurs)
- Value: peer learning, problem solving, mutual recognition
- Best for: SaaS, tools, services for that profession
- Examples: Indie Hackers, Designer News, MicroConf

### 2. Customer community
- People who use your specific product
- Value: feature requests, troubleshooting, expansion ideas, advocacy
- Best for: SaaS, courses, products with deep usage
- Examples: Notion Champions, Figma Friends, Slack-using-Slack communities

### 3. Topic / interest community
- People interested in a topic regardless of profession or product
- Value: shared passion, learning together, discovering new resources
- Best for: media, content, education businesses
- Examples: r/Buffett, Tim Ferriss show forum, book-related communities

Choose ONE archetype. Trying to be all three results in being none.

## Platform selection — what trade-offs you're making

### Slack / Discord
- Strengths: real-time, familiar to most, easy onboarding
- Weaknesses: chronological feed loses context, search is poor, free tier limits
- Best for: small (under 1,000 members), high-engagement communities

### Circle / Mighty Networks / Skool
- Strengths: dedicated community platforms with built-in courses, events, mobile apps
- Weaknesses: cost ($50-300/mo), less familiar UX
- Best for: paid communities, education businesses, larger audiences

### Forum software (Discourse, Lemmy)
- Strengths: searchable, threaded discussions, durable knowledge
- Weaknesses: requires habit shift from chat, harder to grow
- Best for: SaaS customer communities, long-form discussion topics

### Reddit / subreddit
- Strengths: built-in audience, anonymous-friendly, free
- Weaknesses: you don't control it, members aren't yours, terms of service risk
- Best for: top-of-funnel, complementary to owned community

### Facebook Group
- Strengths: largest reach, free, where many already are
- Weaknesses: Facebook controls reach, algorithm-dependent
- Best for: B2C with older audience, hobby/topic communities

The brain's recommendation: start with the platform members already use; switch only when growth justifies the friction. Don't start a community on a platform requiring a behavior change.

## The first 100 members — manual curation

Don't open invites until you have:
- A clear value proposition (one sentence)
- A specific archetype (practitioner OR customer OR topic)
- A weekly ritual planned (call, AMA, prompt, discussion)
- A welcome ritual for new members (intro, first task, peer connection)
- Operator commitment to be present daily for the first 90 days

For the first 100:
- Personally invite people you already have relationships with (existing customers, audience members)
- Vet each invite — would this person create value for others?
- Onboard each new member personally (1:1 welcome message or call)
- Make introductions between members yourself (catalyze the network effect)
- Set the cultural norms by example (how you respond IS the culture)

This is hard work. It's also the only thing that separates communities from chat rooms. Skip it at your peril.

## Rituals that work

Recurring touchpoints give members a reason to return and a context for participation.

### Weekly:
- "Show your work" Friday — members post what they made/did
- Open Q&A or office hours — operator answers anything
- Theme of the week — focused discussion topic with prompt
- Members-only newsletter recap

### Monthly:
- Featured member spotlight (interview format)
- "Quarterly results" sharing (numbers, lessons)
- Cohort kickoff for time-bounded subgroups
- Live virtual event (workshop, masterclass, AMA)

### Annual:
- In-person or virtual conference / summit
- Annual report on community accomplishments
- Awards/recognition for top contributors
- Major theme or initiative

Rituals matter because they make participation predictable, not optional. Members plan around them.

## The engagement pyramid — most members never post

In any community of N members:
- 1% create new content / start discussions
- 9% comment / react on existing content
- 90% lurk (read but don't engage)

This is normal and healthy. Don't try to "activate" the 90% with prompts and pressure — most are getting value from lurking. Focus on:
- Making the 1% feel recognized and supported
- Giving the 9% easy ways to engage (reactions, quick replies, polls)
- Ensuring the 90% find value in the content the 1% creates

Designing for the lurking majority while serving the active minority is the community-design challenge.

## Monetization options

### Free community + paid product
- Most common pattern
- Community generates demand for paid offering
- Conversion: 1-5% of community → paid customer over time
- Watch: avoid feeling salesy in the community itself

### Paid community ($10-100/mo)
- Filters for committed members
- Sustainable revenue model
- Examples: Trends.co, Lenny's Newsletter community, Demand Curve community
- Requires significant differentiated value

### Tiered community
- Free tier (open access) + paid tier (mastermind, advanced content, direct access)
- 1-5% conversion to paid
- Risk: paid tier feels like premium "good" stuff, free tier feels like leftovers

### Community as customer acquisition
- The community IS the marketing
- Members refer other members
- Network effects compound
- Best for: education businesses, courses, info-products

The brain's `business.feasibility` analyzer models community lifecycle separately from acquisition because the LTV math is different (longer ramp, higher retention).

## Moderation and culture maintenance

Communities die from neglect or from the wrong members, not from too little engagement.

### Hard rules (publish and enforce):
- No spam / self-promotion outside designated channels
- No personal attacks or harassment
- No off-topic content (define topic clearly)
- No information that violates members' confidentiality

### Soft norms (model by example):
- Substantive over performative
- Specific over generic
- Helpful over impressive
- Curious over critical

### Moderator team:
- Below 500 members: operator + 1-2 trusted volunteers
- 500-2,000 members: 2-3 paid or compensated moderators
- 2,000+ members: dedicated community manager + part-time moderators

### Conflict patterns to watch for:
- Member contradicting operator (allow disagreement; respect maintains)
- Cliques forming (acknowledge; integrate; or split clearly)
- "Senior" vs "newcomer" tension (welcome rituals help)
- Off-topic drift (gentle redirect; not punitive)
- Promotional creep (firm but kind enforcement)

The brain monitors for moderation events via `social.queueResponse` and `social.triageCrisis` — both repurpose the social-platform engagement patterns for community contexts.

## What kills communities

1. **Operator stops showing up** — community withers within 2-4 weeks of host absence
2. **Off-topic drift** — community becomes a venue for everything; loses its niche
3. **Member acquisition over member curation** — wrong members destroy culture faster than right members build it
4. **Algorithm-dependent platforms changing reach** — Facebook reach collapses, Discord changes pricing
5. **Toxic member tolerated too long** — community remembers the absence of consequences

The brain's `customer-retention` patterns apply but with one critical difference: in communities, member loss is asymmetric. A respected member leaving signals decline to others; a problem member staying signals decline too. Both compound.

## Growing past 1,000 members

The transition from "community" to "community of communities" happens around 1,000-2,000 members. At that scale:
- Single-channel chat breaks (signal drowns in noise)
- One-host model breaks (operator can't be present everywhere)
- Default culture stops working (subcultures form by interest, region, experience level)

Responses:
- Subdivide into topic channels / forums / sub-communities
- Recruit and train co-leaders (chapter heads, regional leads, topic moderators)
- Document culture explicitly (welcome message, member guide, norm document)
- Build for the "first 30 minutes" experience for new joiners
- Create paths for advanced members to graduate to leadership

The brain's `agent-coordination` patterns at this scale start applying to human coordination patterns — same structure (parallel teams, async-first, async-decisions, written-context), different participants.

## What the brain MUST NOT do

- Auto-post in communities on operator's behalf (community trust is personal)
- DM members without their initiation or explicit consent (universally creepy)
- Use community member data outside the community (consent matters)
- Run engagement metrics as performance KPIs (Goodhart's law in real time)
- Auto-onboard "VIP" members (manipulative; degrades community equity)

## What "good" looks like

For a community at month 12:
- 100-500 highly engaged members (depending on niche size)
- Weekly ritual happening consistently for 50+ weeks
- 5-10% of members create regular content
- Member-to-member connections forming weekly
- Operator role: facilitator more than originator
- 2-5 community-originating outcomes per quarter (introductions, collaborations, jobs, partnerships)
- Clear "this community has its own life" feeling

The community that fails: 2,000 members in Discord, 5 active in any week, operator posting alone, no rituals, no culture, no path for new members. The community that succeeds: 200 members, weekly office hours that 30 people attend live, 50+ post per week, members hiring each other, operator increasingly able to step back.
