# Podcast production — long-form audience building

Podcasts are the most patient channel in content. They reward consistency over virality. A podcast that publishes 50 episodes over 18 months builds a relationship with listeners that no individual post or video can match. Audio is intimate; you're in the listener's ears during their commute, workout, walk. That trust converts at 10-20x social-media rates.

But podcasts are also unforgiving. Inconsistent publishing kills audience growth. Audio quality below a baseline gets you unsubscribed. Topic drift loses your niche. Most podcasts die in the first 10 episodes because the operator didn't have an honest plan for 100.

## What the operator should remember

- Don't start a podcast unless you can commit to 50 episodes minimum. The compounding effect only begins around episode 30.
- Audio quality matters more than video for podcasts. Bad audio = unsubscribe. Mediocre video = forgivable.
- Topic discipline beats topic variety. A narrowly-focused podcast in a specific niche beats a "general business" podcast every time.
- The first 30 seconds determine whether someone listens to the rest. Skip the music intro and start with the hook.

## Three podcast formats (pick one)

### 1. Solo / monologue
- You, talking about your topic
- Strengths: easy to schedule, lowest production cost, builds personal brand
- Weaknesses: requires strong personality and clear point of view; can feel preachy
- Best for: thought leaders, established experts, info-product creators

### 2. Interview
- You + one guest per episode
- Strengths: leverages guest's audience, easier to fill content calendar
- Weaknesses: guest quality varies, requires booking pipeline, you're not the star
- Best for: networkers, people who want to learn out loud, building industry connections

### 3. Co-hosted / panel
- 2-3 consistent hosts discussing a topic
- Strengths: chemistry creates appeal, lower per-host workload, sustainable
- Weaknesses: requires reliable co-hosts, coordination overhead
- Best for: partnerships, established teams, banter-friendly topics

The brain treats these as different products with different economics. Mixing them inconsistently confuses listeners.

## Episode length sweet spots

- **Solo**: 20-40 minutes (long enough for depth, short enough for one commute)
- **Interview**: 45-75 minutes (industry standard; gives space for real conversation)
- **Co-hosted**: 30-60 minutes (banter wears thin past 60)

Avoid:
- Sub-15-minute episodes (feels like a clip, not a podcast)
- 2+ hour episodes unless you have a Joe Rogan-tier audience expectation
- Inconsistent episode lengths within the same show (trains listeners to expect a duration)

## Production quality baseline

### Audio equipment minimum:
- USB or XLR microphone: Shure MV7, Rode PodMic, or AT2020 ($150-300)
- Pop filter and microphone arm
- Closed-back headphones (Audio-Technica ATH-M40x or similar)
- Quiet room or basic acoustic treatment (acoustic foam, blankets, closet)

### Audio settings:
- Sample rate: 48 kHz
- Bit depth: 24-bit
- Format: WAV for editing, MP3/AAC for distribution (192-256 kbps stereo)
- Loudness: -16 LUFS for podcast standard
- Peak: -1 dBTP

### Production pipeline:
1. Record locally (each speaker their own mic, separate track)
2. Sync and align
3. Remove cross-talk, breaths, ums (judgment call — don't sterilize)
4. Apply EQ + compression per voice
5. Master to -16 LUFS
6. Encode to MP3 192 kbps stereo
7. Embed ID3 tags (title, episode number, description, artwork)

The brain has `tts.synthesize` for synthetic voices but human podcasts should use human voices for connection. Synthetic is for episode summary audio at most.

## Episode structure that retains

The first 30 seconds is everything. Common structure:

### 0:00-0:30 — Cold open / hook
- Tease something compelling from the episode (key insight, surprising story, listener question)
- NO music intro, NO long branding
- "Today we're talking about X, and what we found will change how you think about Y"

### 0:30-2:00 — Brief intro + episode preview
- Quick welcome (5-10 seconds)
- Sponsor (if you have one — 30-60 seconds, only one per episode below 30 min, two below 60)
- Episode preview: 3 specific things they'll learn

### 2:00-N — Main content
- Solo: structured around 3-5 points
- Interview: discovery → main story → tactical takeaways → speed round
- Co-hosted: news segment → main discussion → listener interaction

### Last 5 minutes — Wrap + CTA
- Summary of key points
- ONE clear CTA (subscribe, join newsletter, leave review)
- Tease next episode

### Avoid:
- Long music intros (kill retention at minute 1)
- Multiple CTAs (dilutes each)
- Promising "amazing content" without delivering specific value
- Repetitive sponsor reads (rotate ad copy each episode)

## Distribution platforms

### Required:
- Apple Podcasts (50% of listening market)
- Spotify (30-40% and growing)
- Google Podcasts (deprecating, but still some traffic)
- YouTube (full video and audio-with-static)

### Use a podcast host:
- Buzzsprout, Transistor, Captivate, Anchor (free), Libsyn, Simplecast
- Choose by analytics quality and price scaling
- All distribute to all platforms automatically once configured

### Submit to:
- iVoox, Podchaser, Castbox, Pocket Casts, Overcast (long tail of listening apps)
- Most happen automatically via RSS once you're on Apple Podcasts

## Discoverability — how listeners actually find shows

In order of impact:
1. **Cross-promotion on other podcasts** — appear as a guest on shows with overlapping audience; biggest single growth lever
2. **YouTube + Shorts** — clip 30-90 second highlights for short-form discovery, full episodes as full uploads
3. **SEO-friendly episode titles + descriptions** — Apple/Spotify search is mostly text-based
4. **Email list cross-pollination** — your existing audience converts to listeners well
5. **Apple Podcasts category rankings** — top-30 in a small category drives discovery
6. **Paid ads** — generally poor ROI for podcasts (consider only after consistent free growth)

The biggest mistake: ignoring discoverability and assuming "if I publish, they'll come." They won't.

## Monetization options

### Sponsorships (most common)
- Industry rate: $20-40 CPM for dynamic insertion, $50-100 CPM for host-read
- Need 1,000+ downloads per episode to attract sponsors
- Host-read converts 5-10x better than pre-recorded
- Network deals (Acast, Podcorn, Glassbox) once you have scale

### Affiliate revenue
- Discount codes for tools/products listeners would use anyway
- 5-15% commission typical
- Effective for niche shows with high-intent audience

### Premium / paid feed
- Patreon, Apple Premium, Spotify Premium
- 2-5% of audience typically pays
- Best for shows with parasocial connection (interview, monologue)

### Lead generation for own product/service
- The most underrated monetization
- Every episode has soft mention of your offer
- Conversion: 0.5-2% of regular listeners → paying customer over 6 months
- This is the dominant model for B2B podcasts

### Donations / tip jar
- Ko-fi, Buy Me a Coffee, direct PayPal
- Works for niche/devoted audiences
- Rarely meaningful revenue but signals listener investment

## Cadence + scheduling

### Weekly is gold standard
- Apple algorithm favors weekly
- Listener habit formation needs weekly trigger
- Most sustainable for solo creators

### Bi-weekly works for:
- High-production shows (60+ min interview with research)
- Shows with smaller audience that compound slowly

### Daily is for:
- News/commentary shows
- Established creators with infrastructure
- Generally not sustainable for first-year shows

### Don't:
- Publish "when inspired" (audience punishes inconsistency)
- Take long breaks without telegraphing (drops kill recovery)
- Vary cadence (train then break expectation)

## The first 50 episodes — realistic expectations

### Episodes 1-10 (months 1-3):
- 50-300 listeners per episode
- Mostly your existing network + email list
- Lots of recording-process iteration
- Don't measure success yet

### Episodes 10-25 (months 3-6):
- 200-1,000 listeners per episode
- Apple Podcasts starts indexing
- SEO starts working
- First cross-promotion opportunities

### Episodes 25-50 (months 6-12):
- 500-3,000 listeners per episode (good shows in niches)
- 1,000-10,000 for shows with adjacent audience leverage
- Starts attracting guest interest
- Monetization becomes possible

### Episodes 50+ (year 2):
- Compound growth begins
- Top guests easier to book
- Sponsor inquiries
- Becomes meaningful business asset

The brain monitors podcast growth via `analytics-interpretation` patterns; flat growth past month 6 with consistent publishing usually indicates topic mismatch with target audience.

## Repurposing for compounding leverage

One episode should produce:
- Full episode (audio + video)
- 3-5 short video clips (30-90s each for TikTok/Reels/Shorts)
- 1 blog post adaptation (full transcript edited for readability)
- 1 newsletter feature
- 3-5 social posts (one per key insight)
- 1 quote graphic for Pinterest

The brain's `social.planRepurposing` (R146.92) handles this automatically when a podcast episode is logged. Without repurposing, you're leaving 70%+ of the value on the table.

## What the brain MUST NOT do

- Auto-post episodes without operator approval (audio quality variability + episode-specific decisions matter)
- Use AI-generated voice for the host's audio (current quality detectable; damages trust)
- Skip transcription / accessibility steps (legal + user-experience considerations)
- Spam podcast directories with the same submission (gets you delisted)
- Generate fake reviews/ratings to game Apple charts (ban risk + integrity)

## What "good" looks like

For a podcast at episode 50 (around month 12):
- 2,000-10,000 downloads per episode for niche shows
- 5%+ subscriber-to-listener conversion (people who actually listen to most episodes)
- 50+ Apple Podcast reviews
- Sponsor inquiries arriving inbound
- Sustainable production process (recording + editing + publishing < 6 hours per episode)
- One concrete business outcome attributable to podcast (customer, partnership, hire, etc.)

The podcast that fails most often: 5-10 episodes published without consistent cadence, hosted in a busy category, with no narrowing of topic, by an operator who treated it as a side project. The podcast that succeeds: consistent weekly for 6+ months in a specific niche where the operator has unique authority.
